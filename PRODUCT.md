# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

NYC building owners, property managers, and compliance consultants facing Local Law 97
carbon fines. They arrive skeptical and busy, usually after hearing a scary number
secondhand. Their job: find out what their building actually owes, and what fixing it
costs. Secondary audience: the same people later living in the dashboard (product
register per-task) to run compliance operations.

## Product Purpose

Fineprint computes LL97 carbon fines for every compliance period, shows the 2030
emissions cliff, and generates cost-optimal retrofit plans. The homepage exists to do
one thing: get an address typed into the input. Success is a visitor seeing their own
building's real fine within seconds of landing.

## Brand Personality

Editorial, exacting, consequential. A broadsheet about money on the line: newspaper
authority, regulatory precision, real dollars at stake. The voice states facts plainly
and lets the numbers carry the urgency. Paper, ink, and one signal red.

## Anti-references

- Generic SaaS landing: gradient hero, bento grid, faded dashboard screenshot,
  rounded-everything.
- Crypto/neon dark tech: glowing purple/teal shaders on black, futuristic gloss.
- Government/compliance beige: bureaucratic portal energy, dense gray forms.
- Climate-NGO earnest green: leafy palettes, earth photos, hopeful-planet cliches.

## Design Principles

1. The number is the story. Real dollar figures, tabular numerals, projections shown
   honestly; decoration never competes with the data.
2. Fine print, enlarged. The brand joke is structural: the things buried in legal text
   get set in display type; the footer is literally fine print.
3. Ink on paper. Light, warm, editorial surfaces (#faf9f6 paper, #141414 ink); red
   (#e5342b) means money owed and nothing else.
4. Quiet motion, alive page. Movement is slow, physical, ink-like; nothing bounces,
   nothing glows.
5. Compute, don't claim. Show the calculation working before asking for trust.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG 2.2 AA. Red is never the only signal (pair with text or weight). Honor
prefers-reduced-motion with a designed static alternative for every animated surface,
including the hero shader. WebGL is an enhancement, never a requirement.
