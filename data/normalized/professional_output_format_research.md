# Professional compliance-output formatting research

How NYC building-compliance consultants, energy auditors, and commissioning
firms structure their reports — used to make Fineprint's building outputs read
like a credible owner/property-manager record rather than a consumer app.

## Sources reviewed

- ASHRAE *Procedures for Commercial Building Energy Audits* (Level I–III report
  structure) and sample Level II reports (SUNY, PNNL *A Guide to Energy Audits*).
- NYC Local Law 97 compliance consultant deliverables (Milrose, AEI Consultants,
  ENPG, Comply NYC, Henson Architecture).
- NYSERDA / Urban Green / NYC Accelerator program reporting style (from the
  Phase 3 cost PDFs).

## Common sections in professional reports

A consistent spine appears across ASHRAE audits and LL97 consultant deliverables:

1. **Building identification / summary** — address, BIN/BBL, gross floor area
   (verified, with the standard it meets), building type, primary use, year
   built, and the records the report relied on.
2. **Executive summary / compliance snapshot** — the few numbers that matter:
   applicable requirements, nearest deadlines, estimated exposure, highest-risk
   items. One screen, no narrative.
3. **Findings, requirement by requirement** — for each law/system: status,
   applicability basis, the requirement in one line, the deadline, the exposure,
   the source data used, what data is missing, and the next action.
4. **Recommended measures** — each measure with the issue it addresses, an
   estimated cost *range*, estimated annual savings, energy/carbon impact,
   priority, complexity, expected useful life, and the source/assumption. Ordered
   by economic priority (simple payback) and interactive effects noted.
5. **Evidence / compliance record** — obligations, who is responsible, what is
   filed, what proof is missing, filing reference numbers, completion status.
6. **Prioritized action plan** — immediate vs 30–90 day vs annual/recurring vs
   capital-planning actions.
7. **Assumptions and limitations** — data sources, missing fields, model
   assumptions, confidence, and what requires professional (RDP) verification.
8. **Source appendix** — public records, datasets, PDFs/reports, page numbers,
   and calculation notes.

## Language conventions

- **Compliance status** — flat and conditional, not reassuring: "No immediate
  filing risk identified based on available records." "Applicability could not be
  confirmed because required source data is missing." Never "you're all set."
- **Risk / fine exposure** — anchored to the records and labeled as estimates:
  "Estimated annual exposure of $X based on available emissions and floor-area
  records." Penalties are stated per the statute ($268/tCO2e), with the basis.
- **Recommended actions** — imperative and bounded: "File the LL84 benchmarking
  report through ENERGY STAR Portfolio Manager before May 1." Measures are framed
  as preliminary: "This measure is a preliminary recommendation, not a final
  engineering scope."
- **Costs** — always a **range** (low–high) with the unit and dollar-year, never
  a single false-precise number; payback in years where known.
- **Timelines / deadlines** — a specific date or a named statutory cycle ("5-year
  FISP cycle; window closes 2027-02-21"), not "soon" or "coming up."
- **Source data / assumptions** — disclosed inline ("from your LL84 filing",
  "estimated") and collected in an appendix with dataset names and page numbers.
- **Disclaimers** — every professional deliverable carries one: the report is an
  owner record, not a filed report or legal determination; figures are estimates;
  RDP/professional verification is recommended before filing or capital decisions.

## Headings and labels to emulate

"Building Summary", "Compliance Snapshot", "Findings", "Status", "Applicability",
"Requirement", "Next Deadline", "Estimated Exposure", "Recommended Measures",
"Estimated Cost Range", "Simple Payback", "Priority", "Action Plan",
"Assumptions and Limitations", "Source Appendix".

## Formatting patterns to avoid

- Consumer-app reassurance: "You're all set", "Great job", "Looks good", "Your
  building is healthy", "Here's what you should do."
- Single false-precise cost figures with no range, unit, or dollar-year.
- Emoji, progress-bar gamification, or celebratory color as the primary signal.
- Hiding a law or a missing requirement because data is incomplete — professional
  reports list it and label it "missing data" / "requires verification."
- Legal claims beyond the data: asserting compliance rather than "no immediate
  filing risk identified based on available records."
